# Amazon Leadership Principles Deep Dive

### Why Amazon LP interviews work

Amazon's interview loop is built on the premise that structured, specific stories about past behavior predict future behavior far more reliably than hypothetical questions ("what would you do if..."). Every interviewer in your loop (typically 4–6 people, including a Bar Raiser) is assigned 2–3 of the 16 Leadership Principles to probe specifically, then rates you against each one independently. You can perform well technically and still get rejected on weak LP coverage — this round carries real, often decisive weight.

### All 16 Leadership Principles

**1. Customer Obsession** — *Tests:* whether you start from the customer's actual problem rather than internal convenience. *Strong answer:* shows you identified or prioritized the customer impact specifically, even under pressure.

**2. Ownership** — *Tests:* whether you act like an owner beyond your formal scope, including for long-term consequences. *Strong answer:* shows you didn't wait to be asked and thought beyond the immediate task.

**3. Invent and Simplify** — *Tests:* whether you look for genuinely simpler solutions rather than defaulting to more complexity. *Strong answer:* explains what you simplified and the trade-off you accepted to do it.

**4. Are Right, A Lot** — *Tests:* good judgment and strong instincts, balanced with actively seeking out evidence rather than just being stubborn. *Strong answer:* shows you tested a belief against data and were willing to be wrong.

**5. Learn and Be Curious** — *Tests:* genuine drive to explore and learn, not just credential-collecting. *Strong answer:* shows targeted, self-directed learning applied to a real problem under real constraints.

**6. Hire and Develop the Best** — *Tests:* investment in growing others' capabilities, not just your own output. *Strong answer:* specific, concrete change in someone else's skill or confidence that you can point to. For ICs, talk about peer mentorship.

**7. Insist on the Highest Standards** — *Tests:* whether you push for quality even when nobody would notice if you didn't. *Strong answer:* a specific standard you refused to lower, and the concrete cost of not lowering it.

**8. Think Big** — *Tests:* whether you propose solutions with scope beyond the minimum viable fix. *Strong answer:* shows you built for a bigger picture than what was strictly asked.

**9. Bias for Action** — *Tests:* whether you can make reversible decisions quickly rather than over-analyzing. *Strong answer:* shows speed *and* a sound basis for the decision, not recklessness.

**10. Frugality** — *Tests:* resourcefulness — accomplishing more with less, not just cutting costs. *Strong answer:* a lean, simple solution that outperformed a heavier alternative.

**11. Earn Trust** — *Tests:* candor, especially when the truth is inconvenient (a miss, a mistake, bad news). *Strong answer:* shows transparent communication, ideally proactive rather than reactive.

**12. Dive Deep** — *Tests:* willingness to get into the actual data/details rather than staying at a surface level. *Strong answer:* specific evidence you personally gathered, not secondhand or assumed.

**13. Have Backbone; Disagree and Commit** — *Tests:* whether you'll voice a real disagreement respectfully, and then commit fully once a decision is made, even if it didn't go your way. *Strong answer:* shows both halves — the disagreement *and* the genuine commitment afterward.

**14. Deliver Results** — *Tests:* whether you push through obstacles to actually ship, with a real, quantified outcome. *Strong answer:* a specific result delivered against real constraints (time, ambiguity, resource limits).

**15. Strive to be Earth's Best Employer** — *Tests:* whether you've made the work environment genuinely better for the people around you, not just for yourself. *Strong answer:* a concrete improvement to colleagues' day-to-day experience.

**16. Success and Scale Bring Broad Responsibility** — *Tests:* awareness that decisions have consequences beyond your immediate task, and willingness to own that. *Strong answer:* shows you recognized and addressed a broader consequence of your own earlier decision.

### The mapping rule

The rule for the loop: never reuse the same story for two different LPs, even if it technically could apply to both — interviewers compare notes, and a candidate whose "disagreement" story and "ownership" story are the same anecdote reads as having a thin story bank, not a strong one.

### "Hire and Develop the Best" without direct reports

This is the LP that trips up individual contributors most, because the name implies management experience you don't have at SDE-2. You don't need to have hired or formally managed anyone — Amazon explicitly evaluates this principle for ICs through peer mentorship, onboarding support, and raising the bar in code review. The honest, strong framing: "I haven't had direct reports, but here's how I've actively invested in making the engineers around me better" — then tell a real peer-mentorship story with a specific, concrete outcome. Naming the constraint honestly up front, rather than dodging it, reads as more credible than pretending the question doesn't apply to you.
