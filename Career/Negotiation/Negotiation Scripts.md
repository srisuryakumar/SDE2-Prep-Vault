# Offer Negotiation Scripts

### First Response to Any Offer (Buy Time)

When offer arrives by phone or email, say exactly this:

> "Thank you so much — I'm genuinely excited about this opportunity and
> the team. I want to give this the thoughtful consideration it deserves.
> Would it be alright if I get back to you by [3 business days from today]?"

Never accept on the call. Never reject on the call. Always buy time.

Before responding: check Levels.fyi, Glassdoor, and Blind for this exact
role and level at this exact company.

---

### Scenario 1 — Single Offer, No Competing Offers

Call back script:

> "Hi [Recruiter Name], I've had a chance to thoroughly review the offer
> and I'm really excited about joining [Company] and the work your team
> is doing on [specific engineering challenge].
>
> Based on my research on Levels.fyi and recent market data for backend
> SDE-2 roles with Kubernetes and distributed systems experience, I was
> expecting the base to be closer to ₹[your target + 10%]. Is there any
> flexibility to move in that direction?"
>
> [Silence. Let them respond. Do not fill the silence.]
>
> If they say no flexibility: "I understand. Could we discuss the joining
> bonus or an earlier performance review timeline instead?"
>
> If they counter: "That's helpful. Let me confirm in writing — could you
> send me the updated offer letter and I'll sign within 24 hours."

---

### Scenario 2 — Competing Offer From Another Company

> "Hi [Recruiter], I have to be transparent with you — I have another
> offer on the table from [Company B] at ₹[X] base. [Company A] is
> genuinely my first choice because of [specific reason: team, tech stack,
> scale of problems]. If you're able to come closer to ₹[X], I'm ready
> to sign today and withdraw from the other process."
>
> [Do not bluff. Only use this if the competing offer is real.]

---

### Scenario 3 — Offer Below Minimum, Dream Company

> "Hi [Recruiter], I really appreciate the offer and I'm very excited
> about [Company]. I have to be honest — the current base of ₹[X] is
> below what I need to move forward given [current responsibilities /
> competing offers / market rate for my skill set in Kafka and Kubernetes].
>
> I'm specifically targeting ₹[minimum] as a base. Is that a conversation
> we can have with the hiring manager? I want to find a way to make this
> work because [Company] is exactly where I want to be."

---

### What to Negotiate If Base Is Fixed

If they say the base cannot change, negotiate in this order:
1. Joining bonus (one-time, zero recurring cost to company)
2. First performance review at 6 months instead of 12
3. Remote flexibility (saves commute time and cost)
4. Additional PTO days
5. ESOP cliff reduction (4-year vest, 1-year cliff → ask for 6-month cliff)

### The ESOP conversation: evaluating unvested equity realistically

Treat any unvested ESOP grant as worth meaningfully less than its headline "current valuation" number, for several honest reasons: the company's valuation could drop before you exercise (private company valuations are illiquid and can be optimistic), you typically need to exercise (pay to convert options to shares) which has its own tax and cash implications, and a standard 4-year vesting schedule with a 1-year cliff means you get literally nothing if you leave (or are let go) before the cliff. A reasonable way to think about it: ask what the grant would be worth at current valuation, then mentally discount it heavily (many candidates discount private-company ESOPs by 50–80% when comparing against cash compensation) unless the company is already public or has a clear, credible path to liquidity. Never let an impressive-sounding ESOP number talk you into accepting a base salary below your actual minimum.

### When to accept and when to walk away

**Accept when:** the offer meets or exceeds your researched target range, the role genuinely matches what you want to be doing day to day (not just the comp), and you've had at least one round of negotiation rather than accepting the first number out of relief.

**Walk away when:** the company can't move at all on a number significantly below your researched market range and below your stated minimum, the negotiation conversation itself reveals red flags (pressure tactics, unwillingness to put terms in writing, hostility to your questions), or the role scope quietly shifted during negotiation to something narrower than what you interviewed for.

### Getting everything in writing before giving notice

Do not resign from your current role until you have a signed offer letter (not just a verbal confirmation or an email summary) covering base, bonus structure, ESOP grant details, start date, and any specially negotiated terms (remote flexibility, early review, joining bonus). If a negotiated term isn't in the written offer, ask directly: *"I don't see [the early review / remote flexibility] reflected in the written offer — can we get that added before I sign?"* Verbal promises that don't make it into the offer letter are, in practice, not commitments.
